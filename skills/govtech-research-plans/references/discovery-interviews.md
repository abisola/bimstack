# Method reference — discovery interviews

Use for: understanding a problem space before anything is designed — how people currently get something done, where the current (usually paper or in-person) process hurts, what words they use, what workarounds exist.

## Method defaults

- Semi-structured 1:1 interviews, 45–60 minutes. In-person preferred (people show you documents, phones, folders of paperwork); phone/WhatsApp video acceptable for hard-to-reach quotas.
- 6–10 participants for a single audience; add 4–5 per additional distinct audience. Discovery saturates faster than usability testing — say so in the method section rather than over-recruiting.
- One facilitator + one note-taker still applies. Recording: audio only with consent; notes primary.
- No tasks, no device requirements — but if the current process involves artefacts (paper forms, receipts, licences, letters), ask participants in recruitment to bring them if comfortable, and note in ethics that artefacts are photographed only with explicit consent and with personal details covered.

## Guide structure (replaces tasks)

Part 2 is organised as **topics, not tasks** — usually 4–6:

1. **Context** — their role/situation and how the subject fits their life. Warm, concrete, recent: "Tell me about the last time you…"
2. **The current journey, walked chronologically** — from first realising they needed to do the thing to it being done. Anchor to a specific recent occasion, not "usually". Probe for: how they found out what to do, every trip/call/queue, what it cost (money, time, transport), what they had to bring, where it stalled.
3. **Pain and workarounds** — what went wrong, what they do to cope (the fixer they call, the photocopy shop, the cousin who works there). Workarounds are requirements in disguise.
4. **Language and mental models** — what they call things (capture verbatim, in dialect, as Q codes). Never correct their terminology; the delta between their words and the official words is a core finding.
5. **Attitudes to a digital route** — only at the end, so it doesn't contaminate the journey account. Not "would you use a website?" (everyone says yes) but "walk me through how you'd feel doing this on your phone" and "what would make you still go in person?"
6. **Closing** — the magic-wand question, anything we didn't ask about, thanks.

Each topic gets: an opening script line (italic), 4–8 probes as bullets, and a facilitator note covering why the topic matters, what to listen for, and what not to ask (the sensitivity line).

## Probing rules (replaces observation prompts)

Standard section near the top of Part 2: prefer "tell me about the last time…" over "do you usually…" · follow the energy — if they light up or clam up, that's the material · silence is a probe, count to five before rescuing · never suggest an answer or a feature · reflect their words back in their words, not official terms.

## Note-taking framework adaptations

- Codes: drop S/E/A, keep H/Q/B/I/+, add topic codes T1–Tn matching the guide's topics.
- Per-topic sections replace per-task sections: "Listen for and record" bullets + a table of key questions with columns for What they said (verbatim where possible) and Notes.
- The journey topic gets a journey capture table: Stage | What happened | Time/cost | Pain (H) | Workaround (I) | Quote (Q).
- Ratings section is replaced by two closing captures: "One sentence: how would this person describe the current process to a friend?" and "What would have to be true for them to do this digitally?"
- Severity table is replaced by an **opportunity table** at debrief: Opportunity | Evidence (participants + quotes) | How it might shape the service.

## Research questions

5–8 RQs framed as understanding goals ("How do vendors currently learn that a licence is required, and when in their planning?") mapped to topics rather than prototype areas.
