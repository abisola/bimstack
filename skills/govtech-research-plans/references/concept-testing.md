# Method reference — concept testing

Use for: a proposition, sketch, storyboard, or early mock that isn't functional enough for task-based testing — where the question is whether the concept makes sense, feels valuable, and is trusted, before build investment.

## Method defaults

- Moderated 1:1 sessions, 30–40 minutes. In-person or video. One facilitator + one note-taker.
- 6–10 participants per audience. Concept testing is about comprehension and reaction spread, not completion rates — quotas prioritise attitude/experience diversity over volume.
- Stimulus discipline: fix the stimulus before fieldwork (printouts, static screens on a device, or a storyboard) and show every participant the same thing in the same order. If comparing variants, rotate presentation order across participants and record the order on the session sheet.
- Recording: audio only with consent; notes primary.

## Guide structure

1. **Context warm-up** — 2–3 questions establishing their relationship to the problem the concept addresses (mirrors discovery topic 1, compressed).
2. **First exposure** — show the concept with a minimal neutral script ("Take a look at this and tell me what you make of it"). Say nothing else. Capture the first 30 seconds verbatim — first impressions before any explanation are the core data.
3. **Comprehension** — in their own words: what is this, who is it for, what would it do for you, what would you do first? Correct answers stated in the facilitator note; do not correct the participant mid-session.
4. **Value and fit** — when would you use this, what would it replace, what's better/worse than how you do it now? Probe the strongest claim the concept makes.
5. **Trust and concerns** — does this feel like government? What worries you? (Data, cost, whether it will actually work, whether the office will honour it.) For Barbados services, always probe whether they'd trust the digital artefact (a reference number, an emailed licence) to be accepted in person.
6. **Variant comparison** (if applicable) — show the alternative(s), ask for preference and why, push on trade-offs.
7. **Closing** — would you actually use this / magic wand / thanks.

Each section: opening script (italic), probes as bullets, facilitator note with the concept's intended reading, what misreadings to watch for, and the no-explaining rule until comprehension is captured.

## Note-taking framework adaptations

- Codes: full set, with E redefined as "misunderstood the concept" and A as "needed the concept explained before continuing".
- First-exposure capture box: verbatim first 30 seconds, before any probes.
- Comprehension table: rows = intended readings of the concept, columns = Intended reading | P's reading (right/partial/wrong) | Notes/quotes.
- Reaction table per concept/variant: Value seen | Concerns raised | Would use? (yes/maybe/no + why).
- Severity table becomes a **misreading risk table** at debrief: Misreading | How many participants | Real-world consequence if shipped | Fix direction.

## Research questions

5–8 RQs: comprehension of the core proposition, perceived value vs current behaviour, trust barriers, and (if variants) the decision the comparison needs to settle. Always include: "What do people think this is, in their own words, before any explanation?"
